Our review methodology

What we check—and what our conclusions mean.

Our process is designed to separate verifiable product details from marketing language and to help readers understand both potential advantages and important limits.

1. Product identity and label review

We begin with the current product name, format, serving directions, ingredient information, warnings, and statements presented by the manufacturer. When the complete Supplement Facts panel or ingredient amounts are not available, we identify that limitation rather than estimating.

2. Ingredient context

We describe the general nutritional or traditional role of an ingredient using cautious, plain language. Evidence concerning an isolated ingredient does not automatically prove that a finished multi-ingredient product produces the same outcome.

3. Claims and evidence

We distinguish structure/function language, such as support for a normal body function, from claims that imply diagnosis, treatment, cure, or prevention of disease. We avoid guarantees and do not treat customer testimonials as clinical evidence.

4. Offer and consumer terms

We review the seller’s displayed price structure, package requirements, shipping disclosures, subscription status when relevant, and refund window. These details are time-sensitive and must be confirmed by the reader on the seller’s website.

5. Advantages, limitations, and suitability

Every commercial review should explain meaningful drawbacks as well as potential advantages. We also identify circumstances in which a consumer should consult a qualified healthcare professional before considering a supplement.

6. Commercial disclosure

Affiliate compensation is disclosed near the beginning of a review and near commercial links where practical. A commission does not convert a seller’s claim into an editorial fact.

7. Updates and corrections

Reviews display an update date. We revise material facts when reliable information changes and correct confirmed errors without silently changing the meaning of an article.

Important distinction: Unless a review explicitly documents testing, “review” means editorial analysis of available product, label, offer, and research information—not personal use, laboratory verification, or medical endorsement.
